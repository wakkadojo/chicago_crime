Synthesizing Crimes

Understanding crime holistically definitively means we need to focus on themes rather than details -- that is, we need to look at aggregates of crime. This requires an apples-to-apples comparison of crimes. Typically, municipalities report their total crime in terms of crime occurrence (the number of crimes per year). This was wholly unsatisfying.

For example, there were XX incidents of Vandalism in 2017 and YY incidents of Robbery. Which is a bigger problem? How much total crime was there? If there are 500 fewer Vandalism incidents, but 500 more Robbery incidents (Robbery being a far more serious crime), has crime stayed constant, or has it increased?

Furthermore, in order to look at crime distributions and inequality, we need to say which Chicagoans are being affected by which crimes. This motivates a framework for synthesizing crime rates, so that we can aggregate crimes of different types, and be able to look at and compare broad groups of crimes. Note that here we are interested in the long-term behavior of crime in Chicago.

  • Focus on what the pieces tell you, not the pieces themselves
    • Look at Total city crime and the three major crime categories: Property Crime, Crime against Society, and Violent Crime
    • Seasonally adjust the crime rates to remove distracting repeated annual variation
  • All crimes not created equal: in order to compare and attribute crimes we weight crimes by how severe they are
  • Crimes affect a community: when crimes are happening around you, you are affected indirectly (culture, children) or directly (a victim)

In order to size up a single crime as reported we used the average crime sentencing from a dataset of Cook County crime sentencing heroically gathered by the Convicted in Cook team. The convictions data for 2005 to 2009 was gathered by working with the Chicago courts over a 2 year period. Note, however, there's a difference between crime convictions according to the Illinois Compiled Statutes (ILCS codes) and the crime as reported via the Illinois Uniform Crime Reporting (IUCR codes). These are not exactly corresponding, since much of the sentencing is determiined in court as details of the case (actual infringement, crime intent, etc.) are elucidated. Nevertheless, we could correspond the sentencing information (ILCS) with the reported crime (IUCR) via another heroic effort.

Below you can see how weighting crimes by their sentencing in fact changes how prevalent different crimes are. In particular, Property crimes are underestimated when looking at frequency alone. Crime against Society is dramatically reduced when weighting by crime severity, which is understandable as these are not Index crimes (serious crimes or crimes with malicious intent). We also show a slightly more granular view of the crimes in the three major crime categories to get a sense of their composition.

Other decisions regarding crime synthesis were handling seasonality and crime distribution among the population. We handled seasonality by looking at crimes via a one year moving average -- this is a simple way to remove both seasonal variation and random variation. We assumed that a population in a Chicago Community Area were equally affected by crimes in the community area. Community areas were used because they have not been redistricted through time, unlike things like Chicago Wards or Congressional Districts, and because they correspond to familiar neighborhoods.

Measuring Crime and Crime Inequality

To build on the above method of being able to compare crimes relatively, we need an objective measurement of how much crime any given person is experiencing in a given neighborhood. This measurement we call Crime Intensity and aims to measure how much total crime there is per person per year, and varies through time geographically throughout the city. From this, we can look at distributions among the Chicago population. In plain english, this means ...

Crime Intensity = (# years served) / person if average prison sentence served in full for every crime reported

Before we dive into the exhibit below capturing crime levels, distributions, and inequality over time, let us develop a little bit of intuition behind these numbers. Lets look at a Total Crime Intensity of 0.15, similar to the Chicago long term average, as an example. Assuming the fraction of the population in serving sentences is unchanged, this would correspond to 15% of the population serving sentences at any given time.

That means, if every crime reported was served in full, 15% of Chicago would be in prison. This is clearly a huge number as nationally in the USA this rate is 1%. This is because, in fact, most crimes are unsolved, and most sentences are not served in full. When we look at the arrest rate for a reported crime (28% in Chicago), the fraction of prison sentence served (75% nationally) and the fraction of arrests leading to conviction (75% nationally), we end up with 2% of Chicago in prison. This is far closer to the national average. Further noting that Chicago is an urban population with a large Black population, which unfortunately has a far higher prison rate than other races, this objective Crime Intensity number makes a lot of sense.

We present two ways of measuring crime inequality through time:

  1. The crime ratio: a simple measure of the amount of crime in high-crime neighborhoods as compared to that of low-crime neighborhoods
  2. The crime Gini coefficient, which borrows from measuring wealth inequality across a population

Tradeoffs

  • Note on const approximation -- examples of gradients in crime, large intra-community crime variation, people working vs not ...
  • Lack of exact sentencing data, mapping nontrivial
  • Potentially missing other factors necessary for normalization (i.e. am I not apples to apples through time), such as shifting demographics, crime reporting policy changes, secular shifts in confounding variables like weather. Also missing things like contextualization relative to rest of USA or developed world.
  • Note: believe none of these is significant enough to affect conclusions
