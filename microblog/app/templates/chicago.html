Executive Summary

Introduction

  • Inequality in America: wealth. GDP growing, markets peaking, but median incomes are stubborn
  • Inequality in America: education -- high school graduation rates, college, problem of high cost of college and impending automation wave (education crisis)
  • Here we look at inequality in a different lens, considering the crime inequality in Chicago, and how that inequality has been chaning over the past 15 years.
  • We are able to do this by synthesizing the millions of crimes that have occurred over the past decades.
  • Goals of crime synthesis
    • Understand how crime inequality is changing
  • Within this framework, we look to understand how crime is now, and how things have been changing over time

Principles of Crime Synthesis

  • Weight by sentencing vs count, discussion of this
  • What's big, what's small
  • Note on difference of ILCS and IUCR: reported versus court decision can differ -- example is first degree murder (ref email from data owner)
  • Principles for crime synthesis:
    • Focus on what the pieces tell you, not the pieces themselves -- major crime categories, seasonality adjustments, major crime areas
    • All crimes not created equal -- in order to compare and attribute crimes in an apples-to-apples way, we need to do more than just count them.
    • Crimes affect a community -- when crimes are happening around you, you are affected indirectly (culture, children) or directly (a victim)
